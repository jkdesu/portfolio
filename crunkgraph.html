CRUNKGRAPH

Exploring identity through playful geometry and cultural intersections. Inspired by Venn diagrams, streetwear, and designer toys across Korea, Tokyo, and New York.

Adobe Illustrator Rhino Grasshopper Computational Design Cultural Research

Project Overview

CRUNKGRAPH emerged from an unexpected connection I made while learning set theory—realizing that Venn diagrams could reflect more than logic. As someone shaped by overlapping cultural identities—Korean American, raised in Korea, educated in Tokyo, now living in New York—I've often felt like a floater navigating fragmented belonging. The intersecting circles of a Venn diagram suddenly became a metaphor for my identity: multiple, layered, and inseparable.

This sparked my core design philosophy: identity isn't singular, it's compositional. Inspired by streetwear, designer toys, and visual systems, CRUNKGRAPH uses playful geometry to map how cultural forces and emotional experiences interact. These forms aren't just aesthetic—they symbolize the harmony and tension of multiplicity. Through this lens, I aim to visualize complexity as a new kind of clarity—where even contradictions have a place.

Conceptual Framework

The project uses Venn diagrams as a visual metaphor to explore how cultural influences overlap and create new design languages.

Research Methodology

Cultural Analysis

Study of streetwear and designer toy cultures across three major cities, identifying unique design languages and shared aesthetic principles.

Geometric Exploration

Development of computational algorithms in Grasshopper to generate geometric patterns based on cultural intersection data.

Visual Design

Creation of custom typefaces and graphic elements using Adobe Illustrator, incorporating computational geometry and cultural references.

3D Modeling

Use of Rhino for 3D modeling and prototyping, translating 2D geometric patterns into three-dimensional forms.

Technical Implementation

The project currently combines traditional design tools with computational methods. Adobe Illustrator was used for initial concept development and final graphic design. The computational workflow allows for rapid iteration and exploration of different geometric configurations, while maintaining the playful aesthetic that characterizes streetwear and designer toy cultures.

Future development will implement 3D modeling through Rhino and Grasshopper for digital fabrication. This will enable the creation of physical prototypes and exploration of spatial relationships, as well as the development of parametric algorithms that can generate geometric patterns based on cultural intersection data. The final output will include both digital graphics and physical prototypes that demonstrate the intersection of computational design and cultural research.
